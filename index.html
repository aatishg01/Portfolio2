Modular Multi-Deformation Mode Soft Pneumatic Actuators

Rowan Robotics & Human Machine Interactions Lab | September 2022 - December 2022

This work is published in Sensors and Actuators A: Physical.

Soft actuators have gained popularity in materials and engineering research for their use cases in handling delicate objects and for their potential biomedical applications. Current research on soft actuators shows promise, but the application potential of a given actuator is limited by its lack of reconfigurability. For example, the popular PneuNets actuator cannot form multi-unit connections without the use of adhesives or mold modifications. In this paper we propose the design of a reconfigurable soft actuator with multiple degrees to mitigate this limitation.

Our design phase for the actuator consisted of simulations of the design of a 3D model, followed by fabrication. The design was created in SOLIDWORKS and consecutively tested in ANSYS/explicit, a commercial finite element simulation environment. The geometry of the design was iteratively improved based on the results of the simulations. The actuator consists of "unit cells" made of silicone rubber and 3D printed thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) which are rigidly connected together using 3D printed polylactic acid (PLA) connectors. The silicone rubber bladder deforms as it is filled with air, and its deformation mode is guided by a less flexible TPU shell. When connected together, these unit cells produce extensile or bending motions. One of the main focuses of the paper is the impact of the aspect ratio of inter-unit connections on the deformation modes of the actuator. By shifting the position of each unit with respect to the following one, we found in the simulations that we could impact the way the actuator extended or bended.

Our experimental design included both the fabrication and testing. The silicone rubber bladders were produced using a three part PLA mold to produce half of each bladder. The halves were then joined together in the final step. As stated before, the PLA and TPU parts were 3D printed.

To test the actuator's force output, we placed three connected units inside a load frame and recorded the force applied as a function of pressure applied to the actuator. The actuator was able to produce up to 40 N of force at a pressure of 15 kPa.

To test the actuator's free displacement characteristics, we suspended the actuator vertically from a custom testing apparatus. Horizontal and vertical displacement were measured for the actuator in multiple configurations. We varied the aspect ratio of the connections, the number of unit cells in the configuration, the angular position of unit cells with respect to one another, and the level of pressure applied. We were able to determine that each one of these factors had a significant impact on the deformation modes of the actuator. In the case of extensile motion, connection aspect ratio increased or decreased the range of motion by 60%.

It is difficult to compare the significance of this against existing actuators since they are not reconfigurable. To test the actuator's potential for gripping objects, two actuators were configured as a gripper. The gripper was able to grasp an orange (210 g), a banana (182 g), an apple (130 g), and an uncooked egg (60 g), all without damaging the objects and while supporting their full weight.

The actuator was also configured into a mobile crawling robot to evaluate its potential for locomotion. The robot was able to move around 11.9% of its body length per stride. While this metric is not easily compared to the literature, its speed of 16 meters per hour is comparable to locomotive soft robots in the cited literature (18m/h).

In conclusion, we demonstrated the potential of a reconfigurable soft robotic actuator. We showed that the actuator has potential use cases in gripping delicate objects and in producing mobile robots. We evaluated the impact of its capacity for reconfiguration on its ability to apply force, its deformation modes, and its use cases. Our next steps include making the design easier and cheaper to fabricate, and adding unit cells to evaluate its impact on functionality and applicability. We have shown that the introduction of reconfigurability to actuators can have a huge impact on the field of soft robotics.

Exo-skin-esphere: A Collaboration between Dance and Science

Rowan Robotics & Human Machine Interactions Lab
September 2022 – December 2022

In 2022 a Rowan University adjunct dance professor, Leslie Bush, contacted my undergraduate mentor Dr. Mitja Trkov and asked to collaborate on a dance project. Trkov works on wearable soft robotics, which Bush believed could look organic enough to create an alien sort of "breathing" action on her dancers. A graduate student and I were tasked with creating expanding and contracting actuators that would be mounted on the dancers' bodies and interact with their motions throughout the show to create these breathing motions.

We used IMU units placed on the dancers' limbs to record their movements. We also created hollow accordion shaped structures out of 3D printed TPU that could be compressed in a manner that looked like breathing. These accordions were actuated by fishing wire inside of them being spooled by a servo. The TPU was also translucent to disperse the light from the LEDs inside the accordions.

We were constrained by a tight deadline and were severely understaffed. We had to create upwards of 30 actuators within the timeframe allotted, and as a result the functionality of the devices was severely limited. We were able to make the expansion and contraction mechanism work on a few devices, but not consistently enough to be used in the final product. The biggest hassle was creating the actuators, which required hours of tuning 3D printer settings. We worked closely with the costume designer to get the work finished, and we were able to get the actuators on the dancers with LEDs inside, although the IMU and actuation components were not consistently functional enough to be deployed on the dancers.

In the end, it was still a mesmerizing performance that I am proud to have contributed to. The choreography was perfectly suited to and enhanced by the design I created.

The Wyrm: RoboSoft 2022 Terrestrial Competition Entry

ENGR 01303 – Junior Engineering Clinic
September 2021 – May 2022

I managed and delegated tasks to a team of six students while we developed an autonomous locomotive soft robot to compete in the RoboSoft 2022 terrestrial competition as part of our capstone coursework. The goal of the project was to create a robot that could navigate an obstacle course as quickly as possible without assistance. Our robot competed virtually against international teams.

We began development on this robot prior to the finalization of the competition guidelines, which created nebulous and challenging design parameters. The design consisted of a McKibben air muscle paired with a 3D printed PETG spring that allowed the robot to expand and contract to achieve locomotion. The device was steered using servo motors that would "flap" to reorient the device and navigated using an IR sensor.

The device was able to achieve locomotion on flat surfaces as well as limited locomotion over sand and loose terrain. It was also able to avoid obstacles using the IR input.

Our team was able to deliver a functional robot on the day of the competition. We were awarded the best Junior/Senior Clinic: Research Category by the Rowan Mechanical Engineering department.

Finite Difference Solver for Modeling Shielding Gas Flow and Build Chamber Temperature in Metal Additive Manufacturing

Introduction

Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF) is an additive manufacturing process that requires optimized inert gas flow inside the build chamber. The flow serves multiple purposes: removing oxygen, carrying away spatter particles, and convecting heat. For my final project in Computational Fluid Dynamics, my team and I created a finite difference solver to model the effects of varying inlet and outlet positions of the inert shielding gas flow on chamber temperature and gas velocity in a LPBF machine.

Design

A 2D CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) solver was developed using the Finite Difference Method to solve the incompressible conservative Navier-Stokes Equation.

The solver used

  • A 0.4m x 0.4m 2D grid
  • Fractional Step Method with Picard linearization
  • Staggered grid for velocity and collocated grid for temperature
  • Blended-scheme of second order for Temperature Advection-Diffusion
  • Modified physical properties of Argon for stability

Boundary conditions included:

  • Inlet velocity of 4 m/s
  • Outlet pressure set to ambient
  • No-slip conditions on walls
  • Specific temperature conditions for inlet, build plate, and melt point

Experiment

The study was conducted in two phases:

  1. Preliminary analysis:
    • 20 x 20 mesh grid
    • 16 cases with varying inlet and outlet positions
  2. Final analysis:
    • 40 x 40 mesh grid
    • Focused on cases around the best configuration from preliminary analysis

Goal metrics included:

  • Average gas speed over the build plate (minimum 2 m/s)
  • Mean temperature in the build area
  • A scoring system combining speed and temperature

Conclusion

The optimal configuration found was:

  • Inlet height: 0.0 - 0.1 m (flush with bottom of chamber)
  • Outlet height: 0.3 - 0.4 m (near top of chamber)

This configuration provides the best balance between:

  • Fast gas flow over the build plate to carry away spatter particles
  • High temperature to prevent residual stresses in the AM part

Limitations of the study include:

  • Lack of eddy currents due to modified viscosity
  • Simplified single inlet/outlet configuration
  • Static melt point position

Future work suggestions include:

  • Using higher compute power to model true Argon properties
  • Exploring multiple inlet/outlet configurations
  • Considering dispersed inlets

Our report provided insights for optimizing LPBF machine design, particularly in positioning inert gas inlets and outlets for improved build quality. We learned a great deal about finite difference methods to solve ODEs for practical applications, and about parallelizing programming tasks for advanced computational modeling.

MATLAB Modeling of Temperature Distribution Over a Planar Surface

Personal Interest Project
February 2022

Introduction

I developed a method for plotting temperature distribution over a planar surface using MATLAB. I was inspired by a simple Excel-based approach shown in a heat transfer class, where cell temperatures were calculated as the average of surrounding cells. I decided to create a more complex MATLAB solution using simultaneous equations to get exact temperature values.

Design

The approach involved:

  1. Creating a matrix to represent the planar surface and boundary temperatures
  2. Constructing a solution matrix of simultaneous equations
  3. Solving the equations using reduced row echelon form (rref)
  4. Plotting the resulting temperature distribution

Key aspects of the design:

  • Uses constants C1, C2, C3 based on matrix size to construct equations
  • Creates a repeating row pattern that is shifted to populate most of the solution matrix
  • Handles edge cases for cells on the sides of the surface
  • Appends boundary conditions to complete the solution matrix

Experiment

I implemented the method in MATLAB, testing it on 3x3, 5x5 and 20x20 surfaces.

Conclusion

The method successfully produces 3D surface plots of temperature distribution. I note that this is an elementary implementation that could be improved to: - Model 2D shapes other than squares - Extend to 3D shapes - Import and model temperature distribution on 3D files like STLs - Account for material properties The full MATLAB script is available on GitHub for further development.
